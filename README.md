## Overview of the Project

Who can actually afford to eat healthy? That is the question we wanted to answer. And we put this question to the test, not just in theory but in practice, accounting for what people earn and what healthy food costs where they live.

To get there, we broke the question into four smaller ones:

1. Are some countries structurally disadvantaged when it comes to maintaining a healthy diet?
2. Do countries that can afford healthy food have better healthcare outcomes?
3. How did healthy food costs shift between 2020 and 2022?
4. Where are costs headed by 2030?

## Key Findings

- **Q1:** Affordability is closely tied to national income. Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South/Southeast Asia are the most disadvantaged because a healthy diet would consume a disproportionate share of income there.
- **Q2:** The correlation between healthy diet cost and healthcare quality is -0.103. There is essentially no relationship. Healthcare outcomes are driven by infrastructure and investment, not food prices.
- **Q3:** Lebanon had the sharpest spike in healthy food costs between 2020 and 2022, driven by economic collapse rather than inflation. Most other countries saw fairly moderate increases.
- **Q4:** Linear regression on 2017-2024 data projects the global average annual healthy diet cost will reach around $2,050 by 2030, up from about $1,150 in 2017.
